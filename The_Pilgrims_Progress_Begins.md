# The Pilgrim's Progress: A Modern Allegory

## Prologue: The Burden and the Call

As I wandered through the bustling City of Man, where the towers of self-love pierced the heavens and the streets echoed with the cries of libido dominandi — that lust to dominate and be dominated by fleeting desires — I found myself weighed down by a great burden upon my back. It was the weight of a thousand distractions, the chains of endless scrolling, the hollow promises of progress without piety, and the subtle shift from common love (the love of God and neighbor) to self-love that Augustine so warned against in his City of God.

In this dream, or was it a vision granted by the Spirit, I saw before me a narrow gate, and above it the words from the Master Himself: "Enter by the narrow gate. For the gate is wide and the way is easy that leads to destruction, and those who enter by it are many. For the gate is narrow and the way is hard that leads to life, and those who find it are few." (Matthew 7:13-14)

A man named Evangelist approached, clad in simple robes, holding an ancient book — the Scriptures — and cried out with urgency: "Flee from the wrath to come! Do you see yonder Wicket-gate?" He pointed across the fields to a distant light. "There is the way to the Celestial City, where the King of kings reigns in perfect peace, where common love flows like a river from the throne of God."

The burden grew heavier as I considered the vanities of the City of Destruction: the endless pursuit of likes and follows, the ideologies that promise liberation but deliver bondage, the migration of souls from truth to tolerance without anchor. Yet, in that moment, a voice from the Book whispered: "Come to me, all who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest." (Matthew 11:28)

Thus began the journey. With trembling steps, I turned from the broad road and set my face toward the narrow path, the map of Scripture in hand, the promises of the eternal City guiding my feet. 

*This repository shall serve as the chronicle of this pilgrimage, chapter by chapter, analogy by analogy, that the truths of Holy Scripture may be illuminated for the weary traveler in these latter days.*
